Personal Profile

My name is Marco Kleimans and as of today, I am . I am currently living in Houston, Texas, with my amazing girlfriend who is finishing her Master's degree at Rice University. I was born in Moscow, Russia, but spent most of my childhood in Buenos Aires, Argentina, where I grew up. I then moved to Los Angeles, California, where I graduated from the University of Southern California with a Bachelor's and a Master's degree in Environmental Engineering. After graduation – and as a result of life decisions and opportunities – I decided to pivot away from Environmental Engineering and started working as a Web Developer at the Information Sciences Institute.

As Jostein Gaarder - my favorite writer and philosopher from Norway - claims, embracing uncertainty and accepting one's shortcomings is a critical first step towards self-improvement and realization. His book "The Solitaire Mystery" has been a hallmark for me, as it narrates the story of a father and son who relentlessly search for their missing wife and mother, while strange encounters with mythical creatures along the way inspire a though-provoking exploration of the meaning of life. This is a metaphor of the search for purpose, perseverance, and determination. It is about understanding that life is not just a destination, but rather a non-linear journey in which we, the travellers, have to deal, accept, and learn from the unexpected.

This story is my banner: I always enthusiastic about facing new challenges that force me to step outside my comfort zone, but I am also pragmatic and always looking forward to learning and developing new skills. I consider myself methodical and organized, persevering and energetic, and both flexible and always well-disposed. I'm passionate about new technologies, and optimistic about the future. Having a multicultural background allows me to analyze complex situations from different perspectives to find the most fitting solution for each scenario. I enjoy learning, sharing, and helping others accomplish their goals, approaching all tasks with open-mindedness and excitement.

Promoting Accessibility Compliance

Aligned with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), accessibility compliance is essential for ensuring that individuals with disabilities can effectively access and interact with digital content, products, and services. By adhering to accessibility standards, organizations not only fulfill legal obligations but also enhance user experience, fostering inclusivity and broadening their audience reach. Furthermore, prioritizing accessibility demonstrates a commitment to social responsibility and equity, ultimately benefiting both users and organizations by creating a more diverse and engaged community.

As a web developer, we are required to comply with these guidelines. The effort to keep all the websites under the ISI domain compliant was monumental, as we were required to review and address issues whenever needed on more than 900 webpages. Not only did we manage to eliminate critical accessibility errors from the ISI website, but we also deployed documentation to help users understand the needs and requirements for hosting accessible websites. Furthermore, this is a continuously ongoing review process to ensure the quality of ISI's websites.

San Diego County Water Authority Regional Conveyance System Environmental Impact Analysis

The San Diego County Water Authority (SDCWA) supplies water to 3.3 million people and supports a $245 billion economy, primarily relying on the Colorado River. To secure a long-term, cost-effective water supply, the SDCWA is proposing the San Diego Regional Water Conveyance System (RCS), which would directly connect water from the Colorado River to the county via canals, pipelines, and tunnels. This project stems from the 2003 Colorado River Quantification Settlement Agreement (QSA), which allowed San Diego to conserve and transfer water from Imperial Valley, reducing reliance on imported water. With increasing costs from the current water transportation system, the SDCWA is exploring two feasible routes (3A and 5A) for the RCS, which would integrate with existing infrastructure and potentially lower costs through multi-use partnerships.

A Comparative Life Cycle Assessment on Shaving Peak Electricity Demand

This study compares two energy smoothing technologies — battery storage and energy use optimization — by conducting a life cycle assessment (LCA) on the Tesla Powerwall II and the Energy Star Smart Home Energy Management System. The goal was to assess their relative environmental and economic impacts, as well as their ability to reduce peak energy demand. The LCA showed that while the Tesla Powerwall is more effective at shaving peak energy demand, it has a higher global warming impact and consumes more energy across its life cycle. The home energy management system was found to be the more environmentally preferable option, though further research is needed to assess its performance in renewable energy grids and its full environmental impact.

Food Production and Climate Change

This report addresses the environmental impacts of food production, focusing on greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and their relationship with agriculture. It provides a global perspective on emissions from different food sources, highlighting that animal-based foods contribute significantly more to GHG emissions than plant-based foods. The report explores the roles of different agricultural practices in emissions, such as methane from livestock and nitrous oxide from fertilizers, and emphasizes the importance of reducing meat consumption, improving crop management, and implementing sustainable agricultural practices to mitigate climate change. It concludes that significant changes in both production and consumption are needed to meet future food demands and environmental goals.

Integrated Fixed Film Activated Sludge Enhanced Biological Phosphorus Removal

The Orange County Sanitation District (OCSD) provides wastewater treatment services for approximately 2.6 million people in Orange County, operating two plants—Plant No. 1 in Fountain Valley and Plant No. 2 in Huntington Beach. Plant No. 1 treats 120 million gallons per day (MGD) of sewage, with much of the effluent reused in the Groundwater Replenishment System, while Plant No. 2 treats 70 MGD, discharging its effluent into the Pacific Ocean. The report explores OCSD's treatment methods, including chemically enhanced primary treatment (CEPT) and conventional activated sludge systems. It also evaluates the potential of Integrated Fixed-film Activated Sludge (IFAS) technology, particularly for enhanced biological phosphorus removal (EBPR), which offers higher biomass concentrations and cost-effective nutrient removal, although OCSD currently lacks strict phosphorus discharge requirements.

Project Slides

Oil-Water Separation Review

This presentation provides a comprehensive review of oil-water separation methods in wastewater treatment. It covers established technologies, such as primary and secondary treatments, including skimming, gravity separation, chemical treatments, and dissolved air flotation (DAF). The report also highlights the use of membrane filtration technologies, like microfiltration and ultrafiltration, and their advantages and limitations in treating oily wastewater. The document discusses advancements in materials used for membranes, such as polymers and ceramics, and offers insights into innovative approaches to improving separation efficiency, focusing on cost-effectiveness and environmental compatibility.

Air Pollution Analysis in Los Angeles

Air pollution in Los Angeles causes visibility issues, building damage, and respiratory health problems. This assignment analyzes particulate pollution using data from the city, covering topics such as particulate statistics, a cascade impactor for particle collection, respiratory deposition, optical properties, and condensational growth under different conditions. It also includes the design of an electrostatic precipitator (ESP) to reduce industrial particulate emissions, which will inform policy decisions to limit air pollution. Additionally, the project examines how the ESP can reduce particulate spread, including implications for the spread of COVID-19.
